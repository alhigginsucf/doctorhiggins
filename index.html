To be human is infinitely more complex and complicated than most of us are prepared to admit. That is, until something happens to us that makes us re-assess whether we really fully in control of our lives. Of all the psychological systems I have studied, Jung’s make the most sense to me. He provides a broader and deeper framework for understanding psychological issues, a perspective which more closely mirrors the vast diversity of our inner human experience.

The need for fundamental change in one’s life is often initially announced as a symptom of inner or outer:

Inner-

  • Sadness.
  • irritability.
  • anxiousness.
  • stuck-ness or malaise

Outer-

  • an affair.
  • job loss.
  • problems relating to others.

Why Jung?

Jung’s analytical psychology can be imagined as a relational psychology, encompassing both the relationship with our own, inner contents, and how this inner relationship carries outside in terms of the quality of our engagement with other people and the world.

Here mind, body and soul form an undivided unity, and space is made for exploration of the full expression of our inner lives —dreams, daydreams, fantasies, intuitions, irrational thoughts, symptoms, symbolic manifestations, spiritual stirrings, altered states of consciousness, synchronicities.

From this perspective, symptoms are an expression of basic underlying emotional needs which are in disunion. For example, perhaps our need to express an intrinsically important side of ourselves —career choice, sexuality, life path — is in conflict with our need to be accepted by our family, partner or friend groups.

Symptoms also have meaning, and their exploration orients the therapeutic path to be followed for their resolution. This idea expresses Jung’s sense that symptoms have a prospective or purposeful quality, in the aim of living our lives more fully. In this case we may ask, “what are these symptoms for”?

Treatment

The first task is to attend to the presenting issues, to help rebuild a sense of mastery over these problem areas. The aim is to restore a sense of personal competency, and create a more flexible and open attitude to our inner lives.

As we create a more related attitude with our inner world, we open the possibility to make room for those more complex and subtle aspects of ourselves to emerge. Jung called this process individuation, a concept which means to become fully who you are. The task here is to grow into the totality of the personality through recognizing and relating to those pieces of ourselves which were previously unconscious, split-off, disowned, and in exile. A conscious relationship with the unconscious. A living of our personal myth.
